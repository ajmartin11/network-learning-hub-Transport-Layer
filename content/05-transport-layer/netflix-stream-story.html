The Journey of a Netflix Stream

From the moment you tap play to the first pixel on your screen — every hop, handshake, and handoff in under two seconds.

Chapter 1 — The request
01 — Your device
You tap play
The Netflix app fires an HTTPS request carrying your session token and account credentials. This travels to the nearest API gateway — often in milliseconds.
HTTPS / TLS 1.3
02 — API gateway
The front door
Netflix's gateway handles millions of simultaneous requests. It routes yours, rate-limits traffic, and passes the request to internal services. All in single-digit milliseconds.
AWS / Netflix edge
Chapter 2 — Identity & rights
03 — Auth service
Who are you?
Your token is validated against Netflix's identity service. Subscription tier, account status, and device profile are all checked before anything streams.
OAuth 2.0 / JWT
04 — DRM license
The decryption key
A license server (Widevine, PlayReady, or FairPlay depending on your device) issues a short-lived key that lets your device decrypt the video. This is why you can't just download and share the file.
AES-128 encryption
Chapter 3 — The decision
05 — Playback API
What to send you
Netflix's playback service selects the best starting quality based on your device capabilities, screen resolution, historical bandwidth, and language preferences for audio and subtitles.
Adaptive streaming

Chapter 4 — Delivery
06 — Open Connect CDN
Netflix's secret weapon
Rather than renting a third-party CDN, Netflix physically places servers inside ISPs around the world. Your video likely never travels far geographically — it comes from a server that might be a few blocks away inside your ISP's building.
200+ countries

Video arrives as a stream of encrypted segments, each 2–10 seconds long. The player always pre-fetches several segments ahead so you never see a spinner.

Chapter 5 — Decode & render
07 — Device decoder
Cracking open the segments
Your device decrypts each AES-128 segment using the DRM key, then hardware-decodes the video (H.264, H.265, or AV1 depending on the title and your device). Netflix pushes AV1 aggressively for its compression efficiency.
H.264 / H.265 / AV1
08 — Frame buffer
The cushion
Decoded frames land in the GPU's frame buffer — a queue that gives the display a steady supply of frames even if decoding hiccups briefly. This is how 24fps feels perfectly smooth.
GPU memory
09 — OS compositor
Layering the picture
The OS compositor blends the video frame underneath the UI — the progress bar, subtitles, the skip intro button — before handing a composited image to the display driver.
Metal / DirectX / Vulkan
10 — Audio pipeline (parallel)
The other half
While video renders, decoded PCM audio passes through a mixer and resampler. A shared A/V clock compares timestamps between the video frame and audio playback — even tiny drift causes lip-sync issues.
Dolby Atmos / AAC
11 — Display panel + DAC
Pixels meet photons
The display driver fires the right pixel values at the right scan lines of your OLED, LCD, or HDMI-connected TV. The digital audio signal hits a DAC and becomes the actual sound waves reaching your ears.
